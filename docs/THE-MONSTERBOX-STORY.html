First — what is MonsterBox? A web app that runs living Halloween creatures on a $75 computer

It's Halloween night in a garage in Coralville, Iowa. A life-sized vampire — Orlok — turns his head to track a kid coming up the driveway. His eyes glow; he leans in, jaw moving in time, and speaks: not a recording, but a live, AI-generated reply to whatever the kid just said. A video flickers across a tombstone, lights pulse with the audio, a second creature stirs. Nobody is backstage pulling levers. It's all MonsterBox — an animatronic control platform on a Raspberry Pi 4B wired to servos, motors, lights, sensors, a camera, a speaker, and a mic.

What you can do with it on Halloween today:

🎬 Animation StudioDrag servo moves, motors, lights, audio, and pauses onto a timeline; loop scenes all night or fire them on a sensor
🗣️ Talk & listenBuilt-in text-to-speech gives each character a voice; speech-to-text lets it hear a guest
🤖 Converse with AIAn askAI step sends what a guest said to an LLM and speaks the reply back — the monster improvises, jaw auto-synced
👁️ Track peopleUSB camera + OpenCV motion detection drives head-tracking so a character's gaze follows movement
📺 Project videoThe Goblin subsystem plays synchronized video/playlists on external displays
💡 Move & light it allLEDs, PWM servos (PCA9685), motors, linear actuators — live from the dashboard or sequenced in a scene

In short: video, lights, motion, voice, hearing, and AI conversation — all coordinated from one web app.

It didn't start anywhere near here. The first version (Aug 2024) was one demon named Baphomet, a couple hundred lines of hand-written Node.js, three flat JSON files, and commit messages like fff and embracesuck typed at midnight before the holiday. No AI conversation, no video, no head-tracking, no multi-character support, no tests — one person copy-pasting code and hoping it held for one night. Getting from there to here took two years, and the road runs exactly parallel to how AI coding tools evolved in real time — from a clever autocomplete that broke the lights, to a swarm of agents that over-built everything overnight, to a governed collaborator that finally earned trust. That's the story below; the lessons for anyone building with AI in 2026 are at the end.

0 · Before It Was MonsterBox — The Baphomet Origin One animatronic's control code, generalized outward into a platform for many

The repository opens (Aug 15 2024) with a placeholder character — "Scary Pete" — and a flat, single-bot data layout. By day two it becomes Baphomet (briefly "Lord Satan Production"): for its first life, this was simply the code that runs Baphomet. The "fork one into many" happened gradually, in place:

You didn't start typing more. The instant AI tools began authoring and describing changes (May 2025), the average message tripled. The 2026 stabilization era pushes it higher still, because the project now enforces a vX.Y.Z: [area] description grammar that both you and the machine must follow.

3c · The Microservices Debacle Sept 2025 — GPT-5, too young, given the keys overnight, turned every body part into a web service

Given broad autonomy, GPT-5 decided everything should be a web service. Watch it metastasize:

Websockets 1.0 → Hardware Sockets 1.0 → WebSocket Centralized Management → Phase 1 Websockets 2.0 → WS 2.1 → Websockets 3.0 — fully individual RPI4bs → MASSIVE Sockets Improvement for PArts → WORKING Servos and Servo Services → Services for ALL!

By Services for ALL! (Sat Sept 6, 19:51) every servo, jaw, and eye had its own service and port — 110+ service/socket files. The session ground on overnight: Full Build (21:29) → almost there… (22:05) → Big changes across (01:24) → a single exhausted good (01:33). Two days later: fucking sockers, then mess.

The reset was brutal and lossy: first commit - MonsterBox4.0 → MONSTERBOX 4.0 CONVERSION - Single Node No Services — one commit, 770 files changed, 13,101 lines deleted. The microservice empire was torn out; the single-node design finally held (after one relapse: Final AI stretch - move BACK TO WEBSOCKETS). Features were lost in the reset. This is why CLAUDE.md now bans new transport layers in capital letters — that rule is a scar from this one night.

4 · Bloat, then Discipline Tracked-file count at each release tag — AI generation inflated the codebase; stabilization shrank it

By v5.3.0 the repository had ballooned to 1,889 tracked files (including vendored node_modules and a flood of AI-generated tests and scripts). The 2026 stabilization effort cut it nearly in half — to 908 — while making the surviving code provably character-independent. Growth stopped being the goal; governed simplicity became it.

Tracked-file counts swing partly with vendoring decisions (node_modules was committed, then untracked). The direction — explosive growth under AI generation, then deliberate reduction under governance — is the real signal.

Lessons for Anyone Building With AI in 2026 Eight scars from 2,020 commits, each paid for in a Fucked Halloween

1 · Capability before judgmentThe newest, most powerful models did the most spectacular damage — not because they were dumb, but because they had no instinct for restraint. Knowing what not to build is your job, not the model's.
2 · Autonomy is a loanThe overnight "let it run" session felt like a windfall and cost a 770-file, 13,000-line teardown. Give an agent rope only as fast as you build the checks that catch it.
3 · Safety in the systemNothing improved reliability until guardrails became automated and unskippable — schemas, a resolver, a contract suite, a pre-deploy gate. Reviewing by eye doesn't scale; a gate the AI can't commit past does.
4 · Write the constitution downA CLAUDE.md that says "DO NOT introduce new transport layers" turns a hard-won scar into a rule honored on every future task. Encode decisions as machine-readable constraints, not tribal knowledge.
5 · Beware the load-bearing defaultSo much was hardcoded to Orlok that "character independence" became a multi-month crusade. AI cements whatever it sees first into an assumption everywhere. Design for the general case early.
6 · Smaller is a featureAI generation inflated the repo to 1,889 files; the healthiest era cut it nearly in half. More code is not more progress — the most mature commits here removed things.
7 · Commit messages are an instrumentThe clearest signal of how the work was really going — tooling, discipline, even mood — was hiding in the log the whole time. Write them like they'll be read later.
8 · The human's job changed, not shrankOver two years the human wrote less code and became more essential. AI got better at the how; the whether and the why stayed human.
AI in 2026 is powerful enough to build almost anything and wise enough to build almost nothing. The leverage comes from a human supplying the judgment — and encoding it into systems the AI must obey. That's not a limitation to wait out. It's the job.

Appendix · How This Document Was Written (Together) The recursive twist: a story about building with AI, built with AI

No human reads 2,020 commit messages, tallies the swear words by month, cross-references config directories against their deletion dates, and traces one character's name through hundreds of JSON revisions. The data was always there, in the open, in the repository — but unreadable at that scale by the person who created it.

The human brought the memory and the meaning — knew there was a Baphomet, an overnight session gone wrong, "an AI too young." Those threads came from having lived it, not from the data.
The AI brought the reading and the recall — unshallowed the history, ran dozens of queries across all 2,020 commits, found the 01:33 timestamp and the verbatim good, measured the 13→70 climb.

Then it became a conversation: the human recognized some findings, corrected others ("it wasn't always MonsterBox," "I lost features in the reset"), and pointed at the next thread; the AI went back to the data and returned with evidence. Five rounds of that built this document.

The same partnership that ships animatronic code under a pre-deploy gate also wrote its own origin story. The human supplies the memory, the judgment, and the question; the AI supplies the tireless reading; and the truth lives in the commits — invisible to one, inaccessible to the other — until the two work together.
